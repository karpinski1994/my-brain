For promoting a mentoring business for advanced programmers on LinkedIn, the best frameworks and narratives focus on transformation, professional ROI, and authority-building. LinkedIn users prioritize logic, proof, and career advancement, so your content should "stop the scroll" with a hook that addresses their specific ambitions or frustrations
.
Here are the most effective frameworks, narratives, and plot structures for your specific needs based on the sources:
1. Top Copywriting Frameworks for LinkedIn
BAB (Before–After–Bridge): This is the ultimate "transformation" framework for mentoring
.
Before: Describe a senior dev feeling capped in their current local role, earning less than their potential
.
After: Paint the picture of them landing a remote Silicon Valley job or launching their own SaaS
.
Bridge: Introduce your mentoring as the specific path to close that gap
.
PAS (Problem–Agitate–Solve): Highly effective for high-ticket services like mentoring
.
Problem: Name the "Senior Developer Plateau" where technical skills are high but business/career skills are missing
.
Agitate: "Twist the knife"—remind them that every year they wait is $50k+ in "opportunity cost" left on the table
.
Solve: Position your mentoring as the relief for this professional stagnation
.
The 4 U’s (Useful, Urgent, Unique, Ultra-specific): Use this for short, punchy updates
.
Example: "The 3 specific negotiation tactics (Useful) that helped a Senior Java Dev increase his offer by 40% (Ultra-specific). Deadline for my July cohort is Friday (Urgent)."
2. Narrative Frameworks (Positioning Your Brand)
StoryBrand (Customer as Hero): On LinkedIn, you should not be the hero of your post; your student is
.
Character: The programmer who wants an international career
.
Problem: They lack the "business of code" knowledge to launch a business
.
Guide: You, the mentor with the authority and empathy to help
.
Plan: A simple 3-step mentoring process you offer
.
Success/Failure: Show the freedom of an own business vs. the risk of being stuck in a dead-end job
.
Star-Story-Solution: This is the best structure for sharing case studies
.
Star: A specific advanced programmer (e.g., "Meet Alex")
.
Story: The struggle Alex faced trying to enter the US market from abroad
.
Solution: How Alex used your mentoring to land a $150k job
.
3. Plot Structures (For Long-Form Posts)
The Hero’s Journey (The Quest): Best for "Founder Stories" or deep dives into student transformations
.
Ordinary World: Working for a local agency
.
Call to Adventure: Seeing a peer move into an international role
.
Crossing the Threshold: Joining your mentoring program
.
The Reward: The "Elixir"—a new career path and higher income
.
The Fichtean Curve: Ideal for stories about the chaos of starting a business
.
Skip the long intros and drop the reader right into a "crisis"—e.g., "It was 2 AM, the deployment failed, and I realized I didn't know how to run a business"
.
Build through a series of "mini-crises" resolved by mentoring until they reach the "Climax" (the successful business launch)
.
4. Strategic Implementation Tips
Master the Hook: On LinkedIn, you have only two lines before your post is truncated
. Use a surprising statistic or a bold question like: "Is your local currency devaluing while your skills stay world-class?"
.
Focus on Logic & ROI: B2B buyers (and senior professionals) want facts over fluff
. Use real numbers: "How my mentee increased his billable rate by 3x in 90 days"
.
Address Objections Directly: Advanced programmers often think "I don't have time" or "I can learn this for free"
. Reframe your mentoring as an investment, not an expense, by comparing the cost to the cost of "inaction"
.
Active Tone: Start sentences with active words like "Imagine," "Build," or "Negotiate" rather than passive phrasing
.
Clear Call to Action (CTA): End every post with one clear, low-friction action, such as "DM me 'Mentoring' for details" or "Click the link in the first comment"
.
