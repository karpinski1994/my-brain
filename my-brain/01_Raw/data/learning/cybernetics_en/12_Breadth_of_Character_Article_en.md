# Cybernetics #12: Why Does the World Follow the Old? — Breadth of Character

**Based on the conversation between Marcin Kapusta and Zenon Dominik Dudek**

---

## What Is Breadth of Character?

Every person operates within a certain range of environmental conditions they can handle. That range is called **breadth of character** (L). It is the sum of two independent components:

- **Tolerance (T)** — how far reality can deviate from your ideal before you struggle. Tolerance is passive endurance. You don't change; you simply absorb the mismatch.
- **Susceptibility (M, from Polish *podatność*)** — your capacity to change yourself to adapt to the environment. Susceptibility costs energy. You have to *do* something.

> **Breadth of Character (L) = Tolerance (T) + Susceptibility (M)**

These two parameters are independent. A person can have high tolerance and low susceptibility, or low tolerance and high susceptibility, or any combination.

---

## The Graph: Breadth Over a Lifetime

Professor Marian Mazur mapped breadth of character across a human lifespan, from conception (T-zero) to death (T-Z). The curve forms a distinctive **"saddle" shape**:

- **At conception:** very high susceptibility, very low tolerance. A single cell must adapt to everything the mother's body provides. It cannot tolerate deviation — even a small change means death.
- **Through childhood:** breadth narrows sharply. The child develops its own program and begins asserting itself.
- **At age 17–25:** breadth reaches its **minimum**. This is the peak of rebellion. A young person tolerates very little and resists being forced. Their own vision of how the world should be is narrow and uncompromising.
- **After 25:** breadth slowly expands again — but asymmetrically. Tolerance grows while susceptibility declines.
- **In old age:** very high tolerance, near-zero susceptibility. The elderly accept a great deal (they've learned what cannot be changed) but will not change themselves or their goals.

The mechanism explains why:

> Children are not very understanding but are yielding. With time, understanding grows and yielding decreases. The dying, at the threshold of death, reconcile with fate, forgive enemies, distribute wealth, give blessings — but brook no interference. They write their will, consult no one, dictate their last will, and leave the heir only to execute it.

## The Four Types of People

Mazur classified people into four archetypes based on breadth of character:

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Narrow & unsusceptible** | Agrees to few things and cannot be forced to agree to anything else |
| **Narrow & susceptible** | Agrees to few things but easily yields under pressure |
| **Broad & unsusceptible** | Agrees to many things but nothing beyond that |
| **Broad & susceptible** | Agrees to many things, and even more under slight pressure |

Two violations follow from this framework:

- **Violation of freedom** — depriving someone of stimuli within their tolerance range
- **Violation of dignity** — forcing stimuli upon someone outside their tolerance range

---

## Old Commanders vs. Young Commanders

The characters of leaders map directly to their position on the age curve.

**Old commanders (endodynamics):**
- High tolerance, very low susceptibility
- Set a goal and never change it — no matter what
- Tolerate enormous losses, even entire armies, to realize the goal
- Excel at resource multiplication and strategic command
- Do not adapt to new information; may happily believe they are being obeyed even when they are not

**Young commanders (exodynamics):**
- Low tolerance, high susceptibility
- Lead from the front — charge into battle, expend energy freely
- Good at tactical, frontline command
- Tend to scatter all resources immediately
- Can be shaped and taught but also easily provoked

The ideal leader depends on the mission. If the goal is fixed and aligned with the interests of both commander and commanded, the older commander wins — stable, unshakeable, efficient at resource management. But if the environment shifts rapidly and adaptability matters, the younger commander has the edge.

## Victory: The Cybernetic Definition

Dominik defines victory not as destruction but as **program insertion**:

> Victory is the moment when you introduce a program aligned with your interest into the controlled object — the opponent's counter-player — without needing to cross them out. They start realizing your interests.

Destruction (grabbing someone by the throat, destroying their industry, eliminating their ability to procreate) does not achieve victory under this definition. Victory is when the opponent unknowingly serves your interests — and the warfare ends in understanding, not annihilation.

## How to Steer People: Susceptibility vs. Tolerance

To trigger a desired reaction (R) in someone, the equation is:

> **R = r × S** (targeted reaction = reactivity × stimulus)

You have three levers:

1. **Change the goal** — introduce a new program into the person (teaching, persuasion)
2. **Change the environment** — alter the stimuli acting on them so the desired behavior becomes natural
3. **Use coercion or encouragement** — apply pressure within their susceptibility range

**Steering through susceptibility:** use law, norms, discipline (coercion) or ethics, motivation, inspiration (encouragement).

**Steering through tolerance:** change the environment or reframe the goal. Do not try to change the person directly. You cannot teach an old dog new tricks — but you can change what tricks are needed.

---

## Example: Football Training

A 10-year-old who loves football is **within tolerance** — give them a ball and they play. Running, dribbling, scoring — it aligns with their dynamism.

A child who comes just to be with friends is also within tolerance — the social environment, not the sport itself, is their alignment point.

A child forced to play football under threat of a bad grade or punishment is operating in **susceptibility** — the stimulus is outside their tolerance range, and external pressure bridges the gap.

Now introduce an over-controlling coach: "You can't dribble. Pass the ball. Execute my tactical program." The child's tolerance drops. What was enjoyable becomes a rigid framework. The child stops coming. A potentially great player quits because the sport became formalized past their tolerance threshold.

The ethical approach: instead of forcing every child into the same activity, offer variety — climbing ropes, mats, ladders, somersaults, different games. Find what each child tolerates naturally rather than forcing them all into one mold.

## Practical Advice for Parents

The hardest age for parenting is **17–25**. Your child's breadth of character is at its minimum. They:
- Tolerate almost nothing
- Resist being controlled
- Rebel intensely
- Cannot reason their way out of it — it is not a logic problem, it is a character parameter

The worst response is to clamp down. That destroys the connection.

The better response: **tolerate the behavior consciously**. Let them vent, let them run wild within safe ranges. Do not only tolerate what suits you. Serve them with reflection so they can understand what is happening inside themselves.

In previous generations, grandparents (exodynamics/exostatics) filled this role naturally. They had high tolerance and low susceptibility — they could absorb the teenager's rebellion without being destabilized. The nuclear family model removed that buffer. Statics (parents) now face the full force of adolescent rebellion alone, with no generational shock absorber, and often lose the battle. Young people then seek compensation in subcultures, stimulants, or the internet.

---

## Final Reflection

> *Life begins and ends with the greatest breadth of character — at the beginning resulting from vitality (high susceptibility), at the end from experience (high tolerance).*

The goal is not to judge where someone sits on this spectrum. It is to understand what kinds of stimuli will produce the targeted reaction in their interest — and to choose whether to change the environment, change the person, or change the goal.
