# Cybernetics #6: Cybernetics for Everyone - What is the Difference Between Autonomous and Self-Steering Processes?

**Speakers:** Marcin Kapusta and Zenon Dominik Dudek

## The Role of the Organizer
Before defining different types of systems, we must understand the concept of the **Organizer**. 
If a system exists and produces something other than pure chaos, it means someone or something organized it. The organizer is the entity that defines the **goal** of the system. It connects the informational track (decisions) with the energo-material track (resources).

Depending on *where* this organizer is located relative to the system, cybernetics categorizes systems into three types: Steerable, Self-Steering, and Autonomous.

## 1. Steerable Systems (Systemy Sterowne)
In a steerable system, the **organizer is completely external**. The system has no ability to make decisions or adapt on its own. It only acts when a direct stimulus is applied by the organizer.

*   **Physical Example:** A flashlight. The human is the organizer. The flashlight (system) consists of a battery (accumulator), a switch (receptor), and a bulb (effector). It cannot turn itself on or off; it relies entirely on the external organizer to push the button. If the human stops pressing the button, the system stops working.
*   **Societal Example:** A fully occupied territory where the local population has no decision-making power and all orders are dictated directly by the occupying force.
*   **Business Example:** A micro-managed solo business. The owner (external organizer) must personally find clients, manufacture the product, and ship it. If the owner steps away, the entire system halts because it has no internal ability to steer itself.

## 2. Self-Steering Systems (Systemy Samosterowne)
In a self-steering system, the **organizer is still external, but the system has the internal capacity to manage itself to achieve the organizer's goal.** The external organizer dictates *what* must be achieved, but the system itself figures out *how* to achieve it and adapts to minor disruptions.

*   **Societal/Historical Example:** Colonialism (e.g., the British Empire in India). The British Metropolis was the external organizer. However, they couldn't micro-manage a massive territory like India. Instead, they educated local elites (internal correlators) to manage the colony *on behalf* of the British Empire's goals. The colony was self-steering, but its goal was externally imposed.
*   **Business Example:** A well-structured company with departments. The owner (organizer) says, "We need to produce and sell 10,000 flashlights this month." The Sales, Production, and Logistics departments coordinate internally to make this happen, adapting to minor issues (like a delayed shipment of parts). The owner doesn't have to micro-manage every step.

## 3. Autonomous Systems (Systemy Autonomiczne)
In an autonomous system, the **organizer is internal** (in cybernetics, this internal organizer is called the **Homeostat**). The system defines its own goals, manages its own operations, and—crucially—has the ability to defend its own existence and steering capability against unforeseen external threats.

*   **Human Example:** Humans are autonomous systems. We define our own goals, process information, gather energy, and dynamically adapt to threats to survive.
*   **Business Example:** A company where the owner and the employees share a unified goal (e.g., mutual prosperity) creating a "Meta-system." The company acts as an autonomous organism that adapts to the market, resolves its own crises, and sustains itself without requiring constant external intervention from a single micro-manager.

## The AI Revolution and Steering
How does Artificial Intelligence fit into this? 

Historically, the Industrial Revolution (Joules) replaced human *energo-material* labor. The current AI revolution (Bits) is replacing human *correlative* (information processing) labor.

For modern entrepreneurs, AI offers a massive shortcut. Instead of being trapped in a "Steerable" business—where you suffer from decision fatigue because you must personally process every piece of information—you can use AI to build "Self-Steering" processes. 

By delegating data processing, scheduling, and routine logic to AI agents, the human can step back and act purely as the **Homeostat**—focusing on high-level goals, strategy, and handling unpredictable threats, while the AI systems self-steer the daily operations.
