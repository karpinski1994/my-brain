## The Two Revolutions: Joules vs. Bits
To understand artificial intelligence from a cybernetic perspective, we can compare it to the First Industrial Revolution. 

The 18th-century Industrial Revolution was the **Revolution of Joules** (energy). It was based on the steam engine and the accumulator. It amplified physical power, allowing one machine to replace the physical labor of a thousand men with shovels.

The current AI revolution is the **Revolution of Bits** (information). It is based on the correlator and abstract processing. It amplifies cognitive power, allowing one person equipped with an algorithm to process information that would normally require a thousand human minds. 

## Defining AI: Information Algorithms
In cybernetics, "Artificial Intelligence" is often considered a misleading marketing slogan rather than a scientific term. A more accurate cybernetic term is **Information Algorithms** (Algorytmy Informacyjne). 

An algorithm, in this context, is simply a program of a steering process. It processes input information into output information. Large Language Models (like ChatGPT) operate similarly to the neural networks in a human brain—they rely on connections (relations) between elements (nodes/neurons) to process data.

## The Correlator and Human Talent
The human brain acts as a **correlator**. A correlator is an organ that gathers and processes information and codes. Its capacity depends on the number of elements (neurons) and the connections between them. 

In humans, this structural density of neurons determines our **talent**. We are born with a specific neural architecture inherited from our ancestors. Some areas of our brain may have denser connections, making us naturally predisposed to certain skills (e.g., abstract thinking or music). The goal of a human is to discover this unique talent and perfect it, rather than wasting energy on areas where we lack natural neural pathways.

Because every human has a highly specific, unrepeatable talent (1 in 8 billion), it can be difficult to find others who share our exact level of understanding in our specific niche. The "Revolution of Bits" solves this by giving us access to Information Algorithms that have digested vast amounts of human knowledge, allowing us to interact with a system that can simulate high-level understanding across numerous domains.

## Does AI Have Consciousness?
If we ask "Does AI have consciousness?", the answer depends entirely on the definition.

If we use the strict cybernetic definition by Professor Marian Mazur—where consciousness is defined merely as **a pathway of correlative conductivity** (a recorded code that allows an input to reliably produce an output)—then **yes, AI has consciousness.** Even simple thermostats technically have this form of rudimentary "consciousness."

However, if we define consciousness as **transcendence** (free will, the ability to independently dictate future goals and adapt one's own underlying programming based on autonomous desires), then **no, AI does not have consciousness.** This transcendent connection between the abstract (bits) and the physical (joules) is uniquely human. AI only executes the processes and goals designed by its human creators.

## Measuring Diagnostic Power
Cybernetics offers a way to objectively measure and compare the capacity of AI against the human brain using "diagnostic power." 

Professor Jerzy Vetulani estimated the human brain has around 80 to 100 billion neurons, with each neuron capable of forming up to 10,000 connections. Using the cybernetic formula for diagnostic power, the theoretical capacity of the human brain is roughly **10,000 to the power of 80 billion**. This number exceeds the number of stars in the observable universe. Achieving this computation requires only about 20-60 watts of biological energy. 

Currently, AI models are nowhere near the complexity, efficiency, and scale of a biological human brain.

## Will AI Take Over Humanity?
Many fear that AI will gain consciousness, go rogue, and enslave humanity (like in sci-fi movies). Cybernetics dismisses this fear.

AI is a tool, like a hammer, a wheel, or a steam engine. A tool cannot take over the world on its own. If a hammer is used as a weapon, the fault lies with the human wielding it. If an AI acts maliciously, it is because a human programmed or prompted it to do so.

Furthermore, AI lacks true **autonomy**. In cybernetics, an autonomous system requires three elements:
1. An Accumulator (energy storage)
2. A Correlator (information processing)
3. A **Homeostat** (an organ that defends the system's ability to steer itself and maintains its functional equilibrium against external threats).

AI has a correlator, but it does not possess a homeostat anywhere near the complexity required to establish true autonomy. If an AI is unplugged or its parameters are changed by a human, it cannot defend itself. 

## Conclusion
We should not fear AI. Instead, we should adopt the mindset: **"If something can do a task for you, let it."** 

We should delegate mundane, repetitive cognitive tasks to Information Algorithms, freeing up our time and energy to focus on what makes us truly unique—our specific, unrepeatable human talents.
