# Cybernetics #1: How to Predict and Steer the Future? (Introduction)

**Speakers:** Marcin Kapusta and Zenon Dominik Dudek

## Introduction to Social Cybernetics
This episode serves as an introduction to social cybernetics, general cybernetics, and meta-cybernetics. The goal is to define basic and foundational concepts so that anyone can understand what cybernetics is and apply it in their daily actions and work. While one speaker struggles to move from theory to practice, the other struggles to move from practice to theory, creating a productive middle ground for this discussion.

## The Origins of Cybernetics
At its core, **cybernetics is the science of steering** (or control).

Historically, the science dates back to Ancient Greece and Hellenistic civilization. The term comes from the Greek word *kybernetes* (steersman or helmsman). It was created to understand how to steer a ship or a state—essentially, steering in navigation or steering in the management of a social system (like a state or community).

During the Hellenistic period, the concept of steering and striving toward goals was systematized into a science. Even though the major scientific revolution occurred much later during the Enlightenment, the foundational methodology of assigning specific meanings to words, distinguishing between the defining term and the defined concept, and maintaining semantic order originated in ancient Greece.

## Cybernetics in the Roman Empire
The Romans took the Greek foundations and applied them practically, particularly in the context of warfare, imperialism, and social control through force. They translated planning into concrete social phenomena. 

For example, the Romans invented the sidewalk curb. This wasn't just for aesthetics; it was designed so that marching legionaries could feel the edge of the road with their sandals. This allowed them to focus entirely on marching rather than looking down to check if they were on the path. This simple optimization is a perfect example of cybernetics: a small improvement that successfully steered the behavior and energy efficiency of the army.

## Re-emergence During World War I
After a long period of relative stagnation, cybernetics resurfaced strongly in the early 20th century, particularly during World War I. Prior to this, warfare was essentially planned on a 2D map. 

The invention of the airplane completely changed the paradigm. Not only did it add a third dimension (altitude), but it introduced a rapidly changing dynamic. Anti-aircraft gunners faced a new problem: they couldn't just aim at where the plane *was*; they had to predict where the plane *would be* in the future so that the projectile and the plane would meet in time and space.

Classical physics is based on the axiom that the present state depends on past causes. If you want to describe a present state, you look at the causes that led to it. However, this approach struggled to effectively predict future states when overwhelmed with variables (e.g., ballistics, Coriolis force, gas dynamics, humidity, aircraft speed, pilot reaction time). There was too much information to process manually.

Cybernetics stepped in to solve the problem of processing and weighing all this information to produce a single, actionable output (e.g., the precise settings for an artillery gun) that a human operator could easily understand and execute.

## The Polish School of Cybernetics & Professor Marian Mazur
During World War II, the need for advanced computing and steering mechanisms accelerated. However, Poland developed its own unique and highly regarded branch of cybernetics, largely thanks to **Professor Marian Mazur**.

Mazur began developing his ideas around 1939. By 1944, he had advanced theories on cybernetics, but his work was tragicly destroyed during the Warsaw Uprising. After the war, under the Soviet-imposed communist regime, cybernetics was banned and labeled a "bourgeois pseudo-science." 

Mazur had to develop his ideas in private until the 1960s, when the military recognized that without cybernetics, they could not effectively defend themselves. This opened a window for Mazur to publish his groundbreaking works, including his theories on systems and the qualitative theory of information.

### Unifying Scientific Language
One of Mazur's greatest achievements was his attempt to unify the language of science. As different scientific fields evolved, they developed their own distinct jargon, making it incredibly difficult for a biologist to talk to a physicist. 

Mazur used cybernetics as a "meta-language" or an Esperanto for scientists. He emphasized defining phenomena precisely. He didn't just want to define words; he wanted to accurately describe the underlying phenomena so that a single term could carry massive informational value without ambiguity. This precise, structural language is a hallmark of the Polish school of cybernetics, which leans heavily into social cybernetics (the steering of society).

## Defining Cybernetics
To summarize the entire introduction in a single sentence: 
**Cybernetics is the science of steering.**

And how do we define steering?
**Steering is purposeful interaction.**

This breaks down into two core elements:
1. **Interaction:** Something acting upon something else.
2. **Purposeful:** Having a specific goal or a desired future state.

Unlike physics, which analyzes past states to understand the present, cybernetics introduces a future state (the goal) and determines the methods, resources, and interactions required in the present to achieve that future state.
