## Cognitive Motivations — The Three Types

**Marcin:** So what about cognition? We have our favorite example: the **Traveler**. Someone who likes to go places and see what's there. The **Explorer**. Why do they go? To see what's there. This is the cognitive motivation of someone who always wants to look around the corner, see what's beyond the next bend. The Explorer sees things at the level of philosophy — "oh, nice, interesting." They have **exploration motivation**. In business, this person is great for traveling the world and seeing what other companies in our segment are doing. That's it.

Then we have **Classification**. Once the Explorer has brought back information about companies in our segment, we need to classify: what elements do these companies consist of? Just because someone shares a point in common doesn't mean they're identical. Someone with classification talent comes, makes folders, describes everything neatly — what elements make up these companies. "Company A makes nails, Company B hammers nails, Company C ships nails."

Then we have **Explication**. Once one person told us what exists, another classified the elements, the third connects everything with relationships. Company A only makes nails, Company B produces and hammers nails, Company C hammers and ships nails. Only after the work of these three people — each with a preferential talent — can we determine the structure of our competition: what companies exist, what elements they consist of, how they're connected, and how together they create the structural picture.

Notice I arranged this very simply to show that at this level there are **no decisions**. The Explorer explored — didn't decide anything. The Classifier classified — didn't decide anything. The Explicator connected everything — didn't set any goal. These people are essential but very often overlooked.

From the perspective of someone focused on execution, the person who does the cognitive work is just that smiley face. Only one person in my workshops ever recognized they had such a person in their company. Most people don't see the need for cognitive-motivation people alongside decision-motivation people. Only two legs let us stand comfortably, walk comfortably, ride a bike. You need both.

**Zenon:** This is where **Kosecki's logic gate** becomes essential. It best explains how cognitive and decision processes are inseparably linked.

The logic gate defines:
- Input state = System X — we must know what X is
- Elements X1, X2 = classification
- Relations between elements = information in set X
- That's the **cognitive process**

Then the **decision process** is everything that's not set X:
- **Postulation** = defining set Y (the goal)
- To define set Y, we must specify its elements and how they relate — an analogous process to cognition, but for what *should be*
- Then we have the **method** — the transformation from X to Y (operations)
- And **substrate** — the starting point (what we already know) — determining whether it's sufficient or we need additional systems

The logic gate gives the most complete picture of the control process: input and output. Resources at input, goal at output, connected by an operation (code) transforming input state to output state.

The common error of confusing goals with methods comes from this: the cognitive process gives us set X, while the decision process gives us both set Y AND the path to it (the method). People who lack decision-making motivation need one precise, concrete program — one tact. They see only the task within the larger work. Because they lack strategic talents, we can't just show them the final goal and say "go." They can't envision the goal at a strategic level. ## The General Theory of Motivation — All 12 Types

**Marcin:** Now we move to the **general theory of motivation** and the full matrix. We have 6 sub-processes of the control process, let me recap:

1. **Exploration**
2. **Classification**
3. **Explication**
4. **Postulation**
5. **Optimization**
6. **Realization**

These 6 processes can be triggered in two ways: by **external influence** (something appears in our environment, we seize the opportunity because it aligns with our character and we want to reach happiness) or by **internal reflection** (we start the cycle ourselves through feedback loops).

So we took this criterion for motivation: is it from **inside** or **outside**?

For **decision motivations**:
- **Internal postulation** = **Ideological** — comes from within us
- **External postulation** = **Adaptive** — started by the environment
- **Internal optimization** = **Ethical** — what we want
- **External optimization** = **Legal/Coercive** — the environment forces us
- **External realization** = **Economic** — using external resources
- **Internal realization** = **Vital** — using internal resources

For example, someone who likes setting goals in relation to the environment has **adaptive postulation**. I, for instance, have strong decision postulation motivations, but I'm more interested in how the environment works, the laws of nature and the universe, and being effective within them. My **adaptive postulation** dominates over **ideological postulation**. I'm less interested in my own ideas and more in how the creator built this world and how I can adaptively connect my character parameters to it. I'm motivated by objective reality working for me.

If I understand the laws of nature and use them to transform the world according to my character's vision, I want to surround myself with people who can fulfill the "happy human" checklist and do the things I want for me. A significant part of my decision processes became about making the world work for people so they can effectively steer their environment and themselves — so they can do things for me, and I'll be happy. The necessary condition: they also have to be happy. After many, many iterations, there was no choice — I had to correlate my goal with other people's happiness. I arrived at: I won't be happy if they aren't happy. So even sociopaths become altruists eventually.

For **cognitive motivations**, we split by **meta** and **sub**:

- **Meta-exploration** — looking for connections to something larger. How I as a person am part of a family, how the family is part of a nation. Someone with meta-exploration motivation seeks connection with the bigger picture.
- **Sub-exploration** — looking inward, into the subsystems. A person consists of functionals: correlator, homeostat, accumulator, receptor, eliminator, effector, etc. If we take one person and go sub, we explore what functionals make up a person. Someone with sub-exploration motivation keeps going deeper — atoms into electrons and protons, and further.

- **Meta-classification** — classifying in relation to larger systems
- **Sub-classification** — classifying in relation to smaller systems

- **Meta-explication** — seeking higher-order connections, supra-systems
- **Sub-explication** — seeking connections downward

3. Ethical optimization (internal)

5. Vital realization (internal)

**Cognitive motivations (6):**
1. Meta-exploration
2. Sub-exploration
3. Meta-classification
4. Sub-classification
5. Meta-explication
6. Sub-explication

Each of these 12 elements must be represented. Remove any one of them and the continuity of the autonomous process system breaks.

If I have no ideas of my own and only react to external influence, I'm not autonomous — someone else is my organizer.

If I only have my own ideas but don't use objective reality, the first disturbance from the environment will eliminate me as an autonomous process system.

If I can only connect upward, someone tells me "bring me a book" and I can't tell if it's a meta-cybernetics textbook, a 6th-grade physics book, or a washing machine manual. If I can't see sub — look inside — it's all the same to me. The image of input system X is incomplete, and I can't autonomously execute the control process.

Creating **autonomous** (not just self-steering) process systems requires all 12 motivation types to be filled. If any is missing, the continuity of the autonomous process system breaks.
