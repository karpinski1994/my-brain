here are many scientific theories of motivation, originating from psychology, neuroscience, economics, and management science. Each explains motivation from a slightly different perspective. Here are the most influential ones:

1. Self-Determination Theory (SDT)

Authors: Edward Deci and Richard Ryan

One of the most well-supported contemporary theories of motivation.

It proposes that people have three basic psychological needs:

Autonomy – the feeling of having control over one's own actions.
Competence – the feeling of being effective and capable.
Relatedness – the feeling of belonging and connection with others.

The more these needs are satisfied, the stronger a person's intrinsic motivation, well-being, and persistence.

Evidence: Supported by thousands of studies across education, sports, the workplace, and healthcare.

2. Expectancy Theory

Author: Victor Vroom

According to this theory, motivation depends on three beliefs:

Will my effort lead to good performance?
Will good performance lead to a reward?
Is the reward valuable to me?

If the answer to any of these questions is "no," motivation decreases.

3. Goal-Setting Theory

Authors: Edwin Locke and Gary Latham

This theory argues that the most motivating goals are:

Specific
Challenging but attainable
Measurable
Accompanied by regular feedback

For example, the goal "I will run 5 km three times a week" is generally more motivating than "I will exercise more."

4. Reinforcement Theory

Author: B. F. Skinner

Behaviors that are rewarded are more likely to be repeated.

Examples of reinforcers include:

Bonuses
Praise
Points or rewards
Incentives

The theory explains the learning of relatively simple behaviors well but is less effective in explaining intrinsic motivation.

5. Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

Author: Abraham Maslow

A classic theory proposing that human needs are organized into five levels:

Physiological needs
Safety needs
Love and belonging
Esteem
Self-actualization

Although highly influential, modern research does not strongly support the idea that these needs must be satisfied in a strict hierarchy.

6. Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory

Author: Frederick Herzberg

Herzberg distinguished between:

Hygiene factors (e.g., salary, working conditions)
Motivators (e.g., achievement, personal growth, responsibility)

A lack of hygiene factors leads to dissatisfaction, but their presence alone does not necessarily create high motivation.

7. Equity Theory

Author: John Stacey Adams

People compare their own contributions and rewards with those of others.

When they perceive unfairness:

Motivation declines
They may reduce their effort
They become more likely to leave the organization
8. Achievement Motivation Theory

Author: David McClelland

McClelland proposed that people differ in the strength of three fundamental needs:

Achievement
Power
Affiliation

Different individuals are motivated by different combinations of these needs.

9. Social Cognitive Theory

Author: Albert Bandura

The key concept is self-efficacy—a person's belief in their ability to succeed.

People who believe they can accomplish a task are more likely to:

Begin the task
Persist despite obstacles
Achieve better outcomes

Self-efficacy is one of the strongest psychological predictors of successful performance.

10. The Neuroscience Perspective

Modern neuroscience suggests that motivation is not a single force but emerges from the interaction of multiple brain systems.

Key components include:

The brain's reward system
Dopamine
Reward Prediction Error mechanisms

Contrary to a common misconception, dopamine is not simply the "pleasure chemical." It plays a central role in anticipating rewards, learning from outcomes, and energizing goal-directed behavior.

Which theories have the strongest scientific support today?

The strongest empirical evidence currently supports:

Self-Determination Theory (SDT)
Goal-Setting Theory
Expectancy Theory
Social Cognitive Theory, particularly the concept of self-efficacy
Neuroscientific research on the brain's reward system and dopamine

Rather than relying on a single theory, contemporary psychology generally views motivation as the product of multiple interacting factors, including psychological needs, clearly defined goals, self-efficacy beliefs, expectations about outcomes, and the neural processes that support learning and goal-directed behavior.
