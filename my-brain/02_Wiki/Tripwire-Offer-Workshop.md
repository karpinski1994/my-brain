# Tripwire Offer Workshop

## Summary

A comprehensive workshop on writing high-converting Tripwire offers. Based on Adrian Kołodziej's methodology (16+ years, 4M PLN annual revenue). Covers the full framework: mindset, guarantee, bullet points, bonuses, CTA, price presentation, pain/cost of inaction, hook, and headlines.

## Core Principles

### Mindset & Preparation
- Writing a great offer takes ~2 weeks, even for seasoned copywriters
- Research competitors: browse Facebook Ad Library, study their offers, value props, argument construction
- Do NOT write an offer "on your knee" in 5 minutes — it will fail on paid traffic
- Use the provided script — simplest framework for beginners, refined over 100+ years of direct response

### Offer Length
- An offer cannot be too long — it can only be too boring
- VSL sweet spot: 15–20 minutes (for Tripwire)
- Agora Financial (billion-dollar publisher) runs 1.5-hour VSLs
- Longer VSL almost always increases conversion (2 exceptions in 500+ clients/year over 5 years)

### Offer > Product > Ads
- A great offer with an average product beats a great product with a weak offer
- Without a strong offer, ads won't save you
- The offer is the priority, not the product

### Cohesion
- One single main thread/idea throughout the entire offer
- Do not dump every possible topic — pick ONE strong argument and go deep
- "One specific technique Agora uses" > "Five techniques you can use"

### Language
- Write like you speak — simple, short sentences
- Avoid complex, journalistic, or legal writing styles
- Your offer must be understandable to a 10-year-old
- Example: politicians who speak simply are the ones in power

### Sell Transformation, Not the Course
- The client does not care about your course
- They need a solution to their problem — a transformation
- Reveal price/method too early (before building value) and you kill the sale
- First build the relationship, then propose the transaction

### Visual Formatting
- Bold, underline, spacing — massive impact on readability
- Do NOT make offers look like a "Christmas tree" (too many random bolds/underlines)
- Short paragraphs (1-2 sentences), frequent subheadings, bullet points
- Clients scan subheadings and text blocks — make it easy to navigate

### Customer-Centric Philosophy
- The client is the center of your business, not you
- Tell your story only in context of the client's struggle and transformation
- Build authority, but the spotlight stays on the client's transformation

## Writing Methodology: Parts, Not Sequence

Do NOT write the offer top-to-bottom. Instead, write each component separately in dedicated documents, then assemble.

**Recommended writing order:**
1. Guarantee (easiest, great warm-up)
2. Bullet Points (product description)
3. Bonuses
4. Call to Action
5. Price Presentation
6. Pain / Cost of Inaction
7. Hook (hardest — write last)
8. Headlines (write last)

**Process**: Work in 60-120 minute focused blocks with no distractions. Each component in its own Google Doc file.

## Component 1: Guarantee

### Key Principles
- **Name it uniquely** — never call it "money-back guarantee." That subconsciously suggests the product won't work.
  - Instead: "Ironclad Guarantee," "100% Investment Safety," "Test at My Risk," "No-Risk Guarantee"
- **Reverse the risk** — client should feel they risk nothing
- **Give full control to the client** — "If you decide this isn't for you, just one short email and we return your money, no questions asked"
- **Long guarantee period** — minimum 30 days, preferably 60-90 days or even a year
  - Split tests show LONGER guarantee = FEWER returns
  - Short 14-day deadlines create urgency to return; long periods make clients forget or engage deeper
- **Frame as testing, not returning** — "Test it at my risk for 90 days" vs "Buy and return if you don't like it"
- **Repeat the guarantee multiple times** — in the CTA, in the PS, in the body
- **Back it with confidence** — explain WHY you can offer such a strong guarantee (product works, proven results)

### Example Structure
"We want you to have full confidence in your purchase. That's why we give you a full 90 days to test Product X. If for any reason you decide it's not for you, just let us know and we'll immediately return every penny — no questions, no problems. You have 100% security; the decision is yours."

### Advanced Example
"My 30-day guarantee: Take my system, follow it as described, and if you don't feel 100% satisfaction with what you're earning, I'll return every cent you paid — no questions asked. What's more, you can keep the entire system as compensation for testing it. Where do I get the courage to offer this? My system works, confirmed by real people who've tested it."

## Component 2: Bullet Points

Bullet points are the most important sales tool. They are NOT feature descriptions — they are **mini-mysteries / mini-promises**.

### Rules
- Each bullet is a **curiosity trigger** — gives just enough info to make the reader want more
- Do NOT explain what's inside the product — **tease** it
- Format: benefit + mystery + specificity
- Use numbers, percentages, timeframes — but NO full solutions
- Aim for **20-30 bullet points** (not 4-5). Beginners vastly under-estimate how many are needed.
- One product section can be described from 2+ different angles = multiple bullets

### Formula
> "How [specific action] can [specific result] in [specific time] — [mystery element]"

**Examples:**
- "Why women are biologically programmed to respond to a specific type of male behavior — and how you can use this to spark instant interest"
- "One ingredient available at any pharmacy for under 5 PLN that can naturally lower blood sugar by 30% — but only in one specific form"
- "The secret of the bloody moon that could triple your money in 90 days"
- "How one change in your daily morning routine can improve your memory by 45%"

### Additional Tactics
- Build authority: "Based on secret research," "From my experiments you won't find anywhere"
- Use contrast: "Normal approach says X, but this bullet reveals Y"
- Include page numbers or timestamps: "Page 47," "Minute 23:15" — adds perceived specificity
- Natural human language — write as you speak

### Source
Based on Gary Halbert's teachings (the greatest copywriter of his era, paid millions per letter). Read his original 1995 article on bullet points.

## Component 3: Bonuses

Bonuses often drive the purchase decision more than the main product.

### Rules
- **Minimum 2 bonuses**, optimal 4-6, don't exceed 8
- **Name each bonus like a real product** — professional, attractive title (like a book title)
- **Describe each bonus as a separate offer** — mini history, mini explanation, why it matters
- **Add bullet points to each bonus** — dramatically increases perceived value
- **Assign monetary value** — "Value 199 PLN, yours free today"
- **Create exclusivity/urgency** — "Only for first 50 clients," "Never sold separately"
- **Use deadlines** — "This bonus won't be available after today"

### Example
"Bonus #1: Secret Report — 3 Hidden Stocks Ready to Explode After Recession"
"In this exclusive report you'll discover the names of 3 companies that we believe could grow 300%+ in the next 18 months. You'll also learn why Wall Street doesn't see them yet — and how you can get in before the rest of the market. Value: 299 PLN. You receive it free with today's order. Note: this report is not available for separate purchase and never will be."

## Component 4: Call to Action (CTA)

### Rules
- Never use "Buy Now" or "Purchase" — these have lower conversion
- Use: "Add to Cart," "Reserve Your Spot," "Join Now," "Get Access," "Secure Your Copy"
- Amazon uses "Add to Cart" (yellow/orange button) — tested extensively
- **Repeat CTA multiple times** throughout the offer (after main description, after bonuses, after guarantee, in PS section)
- **Frame as risk-free** — "Click here to reserve your access without any risk — protected by our 90-day satisfaction guarantee"
- **Use boxes** — a dedicated CTA box at the end recapping everything
- **Add micro-copy under the button** — a paragraph reinforcing why clicking is the best decision

### CTA Box Example
"Here's all you need to do: click below to order without any risk. You get [product] + [bonuses], protected by our 90-day guarantee. If you're not 100% satisfied, you get every penny back. Click here to secure your access now."

## Component 5: Price Presentation

The human mind cannot judge if a price is high or low without comparison. Use **anchoring**.

### Formula
1. **Build massive value first** — show the transformation, the ROI, what they gain
2. **Drop a high anchor** — compare to a higher-priced alternative (your own service, not competitor's)
   - "Normally I charge 20,000 PLN + 5% of revenue for writing an offer like this"
3. **Reveal the actual price** — make it feel laughably low by comparison
4. **Break it down** — divide by days (cost per day becomes tiny)
   - "That's only 12 groszy per day"
5. **Reinforce with guarantee**
   - "And you have 90 days to test everything at my risk"

### Example
"Normal annual access to our system costs 2,400 PLN. But as part of this special offer, you can get full access for just 497 PLN. That's less than 1.36 PLN per day — less than a cup of coffee. And remember, you have 90 days to test everything without any risk."

## Component 6: Pain / Cost of Inaction

Make the client feel that NOT buying has real consequences.

### Techniques
- **Highlight hidden costs of doing nothing** — lost time, lost money, missed opportunities
- **Show the cost of DIY** — "I spent 16 years figuring this out. You could try doing it yourself, but you'll likely go through the same painful journey and spend 100,000 PLN on tests — and still might not reach the same conclusions."
- **Contrast inaction cost vs solution cost** — "Less than the cost of one dinner out, you can gain knowledge that could change your entire life."
- **Emotional visualization** — "Imagine sitting here one year from now, with the same problem, more frustration, knowing you could have changed it today but did nothing."
- **Frame inaction as a lost opportunity**, not a neutral choice

## Component 7: Hook

The first 300 words (or first 30 seconds of video) determine everything. The hook is the hardest part — write it last.

### What a Hook Must Do
- Stop them cold — jaws drop, eyes glued
- Trigger an emotional reaction: **curiosity, fear, or greed** (or all three)
- Make them ask: "What does he mean by that?" — they MUST know the answer
- Be non-obvious, often shocking or controversial

### If Your Offer Is Stale, Change Just the Hook
You don't need to rewrite the entire offer — change the hook and headline, and it becomes a new offer.

### Examples
- "The parasite that's using your body as its personal feeding ground, taking over your stomach first, then your entire digestive system..."
  (This was about candida — revealed later in the offer)
- "Did you know you can legally join a Canadian pension plan that pays you $400–$4,000 per month? There's a legal loophole the government doesn't want you to know about."
- "What you're about to read comes from information I received during a private dinner at a location just blocks from Trump Tower..."
- "Soon you'll discover my shocking story of how I finally escaped the 60-60 hour work week grind for pennies, and started making a fortune from home, working just one hour a day, without leaving my bed."
- "One ingredient found in your backyard, scientifically proven to melt fat 3x faster — but Big Pharma doesn't want you to know about it, and it will likely be banned soon."

### For Competitive Markets
If everyone says the same thing, you MUST find a unique angle:
- BAD: "In this video you'll learn how to lose weight without counting calories, without starvation diets, and without exercise."
- GOOD: A unique, controversial, specific thesis that nobody else is saying

### The Agora Model
Agora opens with bold, provocative claims:
- "Your savings are at risk and nobody is warning you"
- A specific prediction with date and place (e.g., "The dollar will be reset at Mar-a-Lago on December 10")
- Combine fear (threat) + hope (opportunity to profit)

## Component 8: Headlines

Write headlines **last** — after the hook is defined, so they stay cohesive.

### Formula
> **Big Idea + Emotion + Specific Result or Threat**

### Rules
- Never write "Buy my course" or "Check out my training" — nobody cares
- A headline is always a concrete promise or accusation or fear trigger
- Best headlines in history were ~7 words (but longer ones can work)

### Examples
- "New tax law in 2025 — are you prepared? The biggest change in a decade"
- "Forgotten stocks in the renewable energy sector that could rise 100% before the media finds out"
- "Experts warn of a global cash crisis in 2020 — here's how to prepare"
- "New supplement from the Arctic that could extend your life by 12 years — studies confirmed"

### Swipe Files
Use proven headline swipe files. Adapt them to your niche:
- "They laughed when I sat down at the piano, but when I started to play..."
  → "They laughed when I spoke French to the concierge, but their laughter turned to amazement when I replied..."
- Take the pattern, swap the context, keep the emotion

### Platform Adaptation
Facebook/IG ads require softening. Write a strong version first, then tone it down for ad policies using ChatGPT ("make this compliant with Facebook ad guidelines").

## Summary: Offer Assembly Order

Do NOT write top-to-bottom. Follow this order:

1. **Guarantee** (easiest)
2. **Bullet Points**
3. **Bonuses**
4. **Call to Action**
5. **Price Presentation**
6. **Pain / Cost of Inaction**
7. **Hook** (hardest)
8. **Headlines**

Then assemble everything into the script/sequence. At this point the offer should be strong enough for green-light review.
