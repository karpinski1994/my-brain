# Gamification Foundation

Core psychological frameworks for gamification: Octalysis, Self-Determination Theory, Flow Theory, and strategies for overcoming boredom and routine decay.

## Summary

Gamification relies on satisfying three basic psychological needs (Autonomy, Competence, Relatedness) and balancing challenge vs. skill to achieve Flow state. Boredom occurs when challenge is too low relative to skill. The Octalysis framework identifies 8 Core Drives — with Drives 2, 3, 5, and 7 being most critical for overcoming boredom.

## Key Concepts

### Self-Determination Theory
- **Autonomy**: Feeling in control of your actions
- **Competence**: Feeling of mastery (XP, progress bars make this visible)
- **Relatedness**: Feeling connected to others

### Flow State
- Procrastination happens when task is too hard (anxiety) or too easy (boredom)
- To enter Flow, artificially increase challenge to match skill level
- Speedrunning technique: set timer, try to beat personal record

### Octalysis Framework — Key Drives Against Boredom
- **Core Drive 7: Unpredictability & Curiosity** — Variable rewards, randomized bonuses
- **Core Drive 3: Empowerment of Creativity & Feedback** — Skill trees, custom loadouts
- **Core Drive 2: Development & Accomplishment** — Batching bonuses, weighted XP, progress bars
- **Core Drive 5: Social Influence & Relatedness** — Team missions, leaderboards

### Key Techniques
- **Narrative Reframing**: Turn tasks into quests (rename boring tasks with epic context)
- **Weighted XP**: Assign points based on cognitive resistance, not just volume
- **Variable Rewards**: D20 rolls, mystery rewards, surprise bonuses
- **Tiny Habits**: "Just One" technique to lower barrier to entry
- **Environment Design**: Reduce friction for good behavior, add friction for bad
- **Dopamine Markers**: Physical stickers, color-coded emojis for visual progress
- **Secret Identity**: Adopt a persona to create psychological distance
