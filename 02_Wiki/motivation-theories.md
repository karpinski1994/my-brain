# Motivation Theories

A comprehensive overview of the most influential scientific theories of motivation from psychology, neuroscience, and management science.

## Summary

Motivation is not a single force but emerges from interacting psychological needs, goals, beliefs, and brain systems. The strongest empirical support exists for Self-Determination Theory, Goal-Setting Theory, Expectancy Theory, Social Cognitive Theory (self-efficacy), and neuroscience of reward systems.

## Key Theories

### Self-Determination Theory (SDT) — Deci & Ryan
Three basic psychological needs: **Autonomy**, **Competence**, **Relatedness**. Satisfying these drives intrinsic motivation, well-being, and persistence. Supported by thousands of studies.

### Expectancy Theory — Vroom
Motivation = Effort → Performance → Reward → Value. If any link breaks, motivation decreases.

### Goal-Setting Theory — Locke & Latham
Most motivating goals are: Specific, Challenging but attainable, Measurable, with regular feedback.

### Social Cognitive Theory — Bandura
**Self-efficacy** (belief in ability to succeed) is one of the strongest predictors of performance. High self-efficacy → more likely to start, persist, succeed.

### Neuroscience Perspective
- Motivation emerges from interaction of multiple brain systems
- Dopamine = anticipation, learning, energizing goal-directed behavior (NOT simply "pleasure")
- Reward Prediction Error mechanisms drive learning

## Other Theories
- Reinforcement Theory (Skinner)
- Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs
- Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory
- Equity Theory (Adams)
- Achievement Motivation (McClelland)
